Geographic Information (GI) Centre

The giCentre is engaged in high quality research and education into the role and design of graphical techniques for exploring and analysing data and disseminating information. We have particular expertise in geographical and temporal information.

We develop the theory, practice and technology that supports information visualisation, visual analytics and Geographical Information Science (GIS), offer innovative and high quality learning opportunities and play a leading role in the international geographical information and visualisation community.

Research

Our research looks at the way interactive information visualisation, cartography and visual analytics help us make sense of the world around us. We take a creative approach to visualisation and interaction design, which combines best practice, the latest visualisation research and our own novel ideas, often testing and demonstrating these with highly interactive prototypes. We develop theory, best practice, prototypes, software and evaluative work. We work with local government, industry and other researchers around the world.

Topics of Interest

Topics of interest to us include:

Story-telling in information visualization

story-telling and narrative construction from fields such as journalism and cinematography into the realm of data visualization design.

Visual design spaces

We examine the design possiblities afforded by modern data visualization developing theoretical frameworks as well as applying the theory to real-world problems such as the analysis of voting behaviour.

Moving objects and flows

We specialise in visual analysis of movements in space and time such as those that arise from transport and migration. Our work involves developing new techniques for flow visualization such as OD maps.

Communicating uncertainty

Much data that we analyse contains uncertainty (e.g. the census). Working with organisations such Willis insurance we develop new visual approaches that depict uncertainty that arises in collecting and analysing data.

PhD Topics

Content of section 2.

Visual Analytics to Support Understanding of Movement Behaviour

New data sources that record movements of people give us unprecedented levels of detail about movement patterns of individuals. Such data are provided by mobile phones, GPS, public transport systems (e.g. Oyster card data, bicycle hire schemes). This project would develop visual analytics software and designs for analysing such large spatio-temporal data in order to understand movement behaviour. It would suit those with an interest in visual analytics and a willingness to build prototype data intensive visualization software. It builds on work currently taking place in the giCentre on cyclists’ movement visualization, transport research, visual analytics design and migration visualization design.

Visual Analytics Models to Support Data-intensive Systems

This project would examine the design and construction of visual analytics systems to support data intensive activity. It could explore themes of uncertainty representation, visualization to support decision-making and collaborative visual analytics. It would suit those with an interest in data visualization and decision making. It builds on work currently taking place in the giCentre on risk visualization and visual analytics in data intensive systems.

Visualization Design to Understand Electoral Decision-making

This project would design and develop visualization to help understand how we make decisions when confronted with a set of choices. Using voting data from political elections, the project would investigate patterns in voting behaviour over time and space. It would explore how visualization of large voting datasets can be used to develop models of decision making. It builds on work in the giCentre and Department of Psychology on voting visualization and decision making theory.

Visualization of Geographic Trends

Geographic phenomena are complex and spatially variable. We can model them in broad terms, but the degree to which our models fit varies spatially, with scale and over time. Visualization offers scope for enabling us to explore and begin to explain these variations. We would like to design, develop and evaluate interactive systems through which spatial models and spatial statistics can be explored - their characteristics, sensitivities, dependencies and limitations outlined and assimilated. Possible application areas include energy, human terrain analysis or the large data sets developed in social science (Society Today, the UK Census) - areas in which we have experience and expertise. But we are equally interested in developing such systems in new areas where spatial statistics may be usefully used. Possible numeric methods include geographically weighted regression, geographically weighted correlation and indices of spatial autocorrelation. The project builds on work currently taking place in the giCentre on visualizing geographic phenomena and spatial statistics. Consider our OACExplorer and geowigs work.

Crowd Sourced Visualization of Volunteered and Open Data

Open data initiatives are associated with a call for open analysis and visualization - but it is not clear whether of not this is working, resulting in many questions to which we do not currently have answers: What are the effects, successes and failures associated with crowd sourcing visualization? How and when and why does this work well, or poorly? Is it efficient? What are the costs and benefits? How is the variable quality and availability of some of these data being accounted for and represented in such visualization? We propose a comprehensive and systematic analysis of the visualization emerging from open data initiatives and the availability of volunteered data that will address these kinds of questions. The project builds on work currently taking place in the giCentre on developing visualization solutions for open and volunteered data. Consider our BallotMaps, PlaceSurvey and geograph work.

Establishing the Synergies Between Information Visualization and GeoVisualization

Maps emphasize the spatial characteristics of data sets. Information visualization tends to emphasize non-spatial structural information. But many useful representations show spatial and structural information concurrently: for example, the famous maps of the London Underground or the increasingly cartograms used to show election results. At the giCentre we have been exploring ways of varying the amount of geographic information represented in our maps and graphics and have developed some interesting techniques for so-doing. These have been used in a number of different applications and domains. We would like to do some creative design work to explore novel ways of combining spatial and aspatial information in graphics and then applying, developing and evaluating these in particular application domains. This will help us establish how much geography can we convey, usefully use or retain with these novel maps and learn more about how much geography we need from our maps and in what circumstances. The work will involve considering visualization techniques developed at recent meetings of IEEE Information Visualization and other key visualization outlets and adding a spatial component. The project builds on work currently taking place in the giCentre on developing and evaluating novel semi-spatial graphics in a range of applied contexts. Consider our Spatial TreeMaps, BikeGrid and HiVE/HiDE work.

Exploring the Evolving Role of Animation in GeoVisualization

Various forms of animation are used in the kinds of dynamic graphics that are being generated to help analysts see structure in their data and understand its complexity. Some cartographic research is beginning to establish the relationships between animation, task and data. We aim to contribute to this work by evaluating some of the forms of animation used in giCentre applications: animated transitions, fireflies, etc. in a range of applied contexts to develop a model and guidelines for the use of animation in geovisualization. The work will involve extending these forms in innovative ways. The project builds on work currently taking place in the giCentre on developing and evaluating dynamic graphics in a range of applied contexts. Consider our animated visualization of flows, PlaceSurvey, BikeGrid and HiDE work.

Mapping temporal trends and their statistical significance

Maps are suitable for showing spatial patterns but not so good at showing change over time. Difference maps between two snapshots in time, animation over time, small multiples and multiple coordinated views are often used. This project will develop and test range of visualisation techniques to help show trends in the spatial distribution of human or animal populations. It will also investigate incorporating statistical significance tests to determine whether trends are likely to be "real" or just the results of the background variation. The project would suit candidates interested in cartography, geography, information visualisation, prototyping, user-centred methods.

Visualising geographical uncertainty

Uncertainty that varies spatially exists in most data and model outputs. An understanding of this may be important for decision-making for flood and other scenarios. Information visualisation can harness a variety of visual variable to depict different aspects of uncertainty and have the potential to provide more detailed information that global uncertainty statistics that are often employed. This project will investigate effective ways of depicting uncertainty for one or more use-cases and user-group. Opportunities to develop such use-cases exist though our partners in the Willis Research Network and may include uncertainty in weather forecasting for the public, supporting flood defence decisions or placing insurance. The project would suit candidates interested in cartography, geography, information visualisation, prototyping, user-centred methods.

Visual analysis of the movement of animals, humans and other objects

Movement data of humans, animals and other objects are increasingly being collected. They are helping answer questions on how animal species interact, how humans organise their lives, what motivates individuals' behaviours and the spread of disease. Appropriate methods of working with such data are affected by the sampling rate. Visual analysis of large volumes of movement data poses challenges, requiring spatial and temporal aspects of data to be depicted in such a way that patterns of interest can be identified. Opportunities include studying the spread of TB in cattle and helping ecologists infer the behaviour of birds from positional and accelerometer information. The project would suit candidates interested in cartography, geography, information visualisation, prototyping, user-centred methods.
